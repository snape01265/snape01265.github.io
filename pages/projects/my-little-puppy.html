Experience and Feelings

Working on My Little Puppy at Dreamotion was my first professional project and a massive leap from my student days. Collaborating with a team of up to 30 people taught me how to work within established pipelines, communicate with other team members with different roles (not just engineers), and deliver production quality work under tight deadlines. And by tight deadlines, I mean one to two days to complete entire features.

Participating in the development and experiencing the full cycle of a game project until its release was quite an experience. Unlike the indie games I had worked on previously, creating a big commercial game made me realize the complexity and scale of such projects. Also, trying a 3D platformer gave me new insights as well as tough challenges in programming that I enjoyed solving.

Shipping on multiple platforms gave me some insight into platform specific development that I wouldn't have experienced anywhere else. Towards the tail end of development, I had the opportunity to try out different fields of work (like DevOps, PM, Localizing, and Game Design) while porting the game to different platforms that I would have otherwise not experienced.

    Memorable Things from Development

    Barista System

    Early in the project, every narrative sequence was hard-coded, including repetitive tasks like triggering animations at certain timings. But as the project progressed, we realized that these tasks were becoming bottlenecks for the sequences that the designers had envisioned.

    Instead of programmers trying to figure out the details for that scene's narrative sequences, I built a system and an editor tool that gave designers (and everybody else) a way to author events themselves. This drastically sped up our creative process, enabling all members to test and iterate on multiple ideas without waiting on a programmer (AKA me).

    What started as a simple tool for narrative sequences quickly scaled into the core of our game's logic. We expanded Barista for use in cutscenes, trigger events, checkpoint progression, and more. Ultimately, it became an essential tool that enabled most scripted events in MLP, streamlining our entire development process.

    Cross-Platform Input System

    To support MLP's release across PC, PS5, and Nintendo Switch, I implemented an abstraction layer over Unity's Input System.

    The OmniInput class handles real-time device switching and key/pad remapping. It features a dynamic sprite mapper that translates raw inputs into platform-specific key sprites. This ensured console compliance and allowed designers to write text in Baristas without worrying about the active hardware. All the UIs (in-game, menu, settings, etc...) would be affected by this system.

    Porting & Optimization

    Bringing MLP to the Nintendo Switch, PS5, and LG TV introduced me and my fellow porting team member to strict hardware constraints that required aggressive optimization. Working with limited memory and specs gave me a newfound respect for older console games. Developers back in the days were using black magics to run games so seamlessly on limited hardwares...

    For the Nintendo Switch, I created a profiling system and helped optimize the base game, ensuring we maintained a stable framerate without sacrificing the game's visual aesthetic. (or at least, trying my best to preserve it) This was a great opportunity for me to learn all kinds of optimization techniques. For PS5, optimization was not an issue, but the TRC was a hassle to meet.

    Beyond raw performance, translating the game to console required tweaking values to improve player experience. We rebalanced the difficulty of mini-games, overhauled the input handling to smooth out the roughness of the controls, and added additional mechanics to assist platforming. The goal was to ensure the dog's movement and platforming feel natural and responsive on a thumbstick, providing a good experience across all devices.

    The difference in platforms caused a lot of issues in the game, so naturally, a quick cycle of testing and fixing was crucial. I utilized CI tools, including Jenkins, NintendoSDK, and PS5SDK, to automate our deployment processes. This allowed the team to run fast, on-device iterations and test sessions, helping catch TRC violations and bugs more conveniently. This automated process increased our overall development speed.
